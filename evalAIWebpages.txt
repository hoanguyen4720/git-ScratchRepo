*** Usability: Will your visitors be likely to browse or hit the high points? ***

Your question gets at a core usability decision: Are people going to explore your site deeply, or are they just trying to grab the essentials and leave?  
The answer shapes everything from layout to navigation to content density.

Here’s a clear breakdown of how to think about it, with Guided Links so you can dig into any part.
🧭 Visitor Intent

The first thing to understand is why people come to your site.
Most sites fall into one of two patterns:

    High‑point seekers — They want quick answers, key info, or a single action (e.g., book, buy, contact).

    Browsers — They’re exploring, comparing, learning, or enjoying the experience.

Your design should match the dominant behavior.
⚡ High‑Point Behavior

If your visitors mostly want the essentials, design for speed and clarity.

Key traits:

    Short pages with clear sections and scannable text.

    Prominent CTAs (“Book now”, “Get quote”, “Contact”).

    Sticky navigation so they can jump quickly.

    Above‑the‑fold essentials: what you do, who it’s for, why it matters.

    Minimal distractions — no long paragraphs, no deep menus.

This is common for:

    Restaurants

    Service businesses

    Medical offices

    Event pages

    Landing pages

🌿 Browsing Behavior

If your visitors like to explore, design for depth and discovery.

Key traits:

    Rich navigation with categories and subpages.

    Longer content that rewards scrolling.

    Visual storytelling — images, illustrations, interactive elements.

    Internal linking to encourage deeper exploration.

    Content hierarchy that unfolds gradually.

This is common for:

    Blogs

    E‑commerce

    Portfolios

    Educational sites

    Community or membership platforms

🎯 How to Determine Which One You Need

You can infer visitor behavior by looking at:

    Your industry — Some industries naturally lean one way.

    Your goals — Do you want conversions or engagement?

    Your content — Do you have a lot to say or just the essentials?

    Your analytics (if redesigning) — Time on page, bounce rate, scroll depth.

    User expectations — People expect certain patterns based on similar sites.

🧩 Designing for Both

Many sites need to serve both types of visitors.
The trick is progressive disclosure:

    Put the essentials at the top.

    Let deeper content unfold below.

    Use clear section headers so scanners can skip.

    Use internal links so browsers can dive deeper.

This hybrid approach works extremely well for modern websites.
